# 4000W
    
Four Thousand Weeks by Oliver Burkeman. When I first picked up the book, I resisted it just by glancing at the book title and thought: Ok...yet another time management book, but to my pleasant surprise, 4000W is closer to a Zen practice book or a psychology book than a time management one. Those who fail to appreciate this book likely lack readings in mindfulness or Zen Buddhism. 

!!!Quote

    I wish I could reveal, at this point, the secret for uprooting the urge toward distraction - the way to have it not feel unpleasant, on something you value, or that you can't easily choose not to do. But the truth is that I don't think there is one. The most effective way to sap distraction of its power is just to stop expecting things to be otherwise - to accept that this unpleasantness is simply what it feels like for finite humans to commit ourselves to the kinds of demanding and valuable tasks that force us to confront our limited control over how our lives unfold. 

    (Four Thousand Weeks By Oliver Burkeman, pg. 108)

## Confronting the reality

Burkeman walked readers across time by visiting medieval farmers' routines and time before timetables, how industrialization and societal progress push us to work ever harder with endless chores and tasks, and his ultimate conclusions are both philosophical and zen-like: the best way to manage time is to not manage time, and to do that one must accept the reality: life is short, is constrained, and we are aging faster and faster as time goes by so confronting this reality just like how Steve Young confronted the icy cold shower when he was practicing in Mount Koya in Japan. 

This seemingly pessimistic view is however resonating. Simply, it is more useful and wise to accept what is true than falsely believing otherwise. Recognizing life is constrained, bounded, and limited empowers us to choose our focus and filter out the noises.

Burkeman, unlike others, did **not** approach the book from a "how to eliminate distractions" or "how to be more productive", but rather Burkeman urges us to think and to reflect and to choose our focus based on subjective values. And while we do the things we choose, we will inevitably fall into boredom and confronting this boredom is much more effective at freeing us from the it than if we were to run away from it.

## Being time

!!! Quote 
    One thinks with a watch in one's hand, even as one's midday meal while reading the latest news of the stock market." (Friedrich Nietzsche)

The opposite extreme of what Nietzsche complained about is living with time but not realizing it. Burkeman referred to this as the "deep time", it is precisely the sublime feeling of timeless elapse that Carl Jung perfectly captured in the first quote. 

So let's examine both sides of the spectrum. On one side, there is the mult-tasked utility-driven way of living, quantifying hours in dollar signs; on the other side of the spectrum, the clock is ticking and we aren't even noticing, we are just living and being present. Honestly, when I read to this point, I felt Burkeman must read or at least was influenced by mindfulness practice or Buddism philosophies.

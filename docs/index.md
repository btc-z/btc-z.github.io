# Less is More


## The Ultimate Resource

### Malthusian Delusion

> "You can never step in the same river twice." - Heraclitus

Malthusian thinking dictates that an uncontrolled population would eventually outpace the ability of Earth to produce resources,  leading to war, famine, and death. Yet, no raw material in all human history has ever run out. Even the rarest metal on earth, gold, has an annual production rate that has been ever-increasing for thousands of years. It is perhaps the biggest mistake for mankind to think that resources are scarce, the only thing that is scarce to human civilization is time, that is pure human time towards producing the resources. 

Why is the idea that "resource is scarce and will eventually run out" so persistent and deeply ingrained? Beyond humans' survival instinct and natural tendency to distaste material reduction, few people examine the underlying assumptions of Malthusianism. With a moderate amount of geology knowledge and logic, one can realize that scarcity is a function of human production time towards that resource and technological advancements to mine this resource.

### A bit of Geology

> "…reason now persuades me that I should withhold my assent no less carefully from opinions that are not completely certain and indubitable than I would from those that are patently false." - The First Meditation, Descartes

The finiteness of natural resources is valid only in its pure semantic sense, but practically humans have explored a minuscule amount of the Earth. Earth's mantle, which accounts for approximately 84% of the planet's total volume, is virtually unexplored. We have only explored parts of the crust, which is less than 1% of the Earth's volume. And even in this less than 1% volume, humans have barely scratched the surface. We know little about oceans (71%), deserts (22%), the Arctic (2.8%), and the Antarctic (4%). Among that 1% of the Earth's volume, we have explored much less than 1% of that 1%. Yet, we still deeply believe that natural resource is so scarce and deeply believe such worry is rational.

### Simon-Ehrlich Wager

> "Necessity is the mother of invention" - Plato

Julian Simon, most famously known for the Simon-Ehrlich wager, made a bet with ecologist Ehrlich that the prices of five raw metals (tin, nickel, tungsten, chromium, and copper) would only increase in the next decade. Simon won the bet in 1990. Total supplies of these metals increased between 1980-1990. Prices of the five metals declined as improved technology and innovation boosted productivity, which allowed less human time to produce more resources. 

Most people who read that "there are 47 years of oil left" forgot to also examine the footnote: "at current consumption and supply levels". Almost all world oil meters and statistics graph has this footnote. With abundance of oil data dating back to 4 decades, we can easily check the oil reserve has been increasing only at increasing speed. As consumption increases, so does supply. A free economic market drives this because an increases in consumption push up the price which lead to better technology in order to produce resource more efficiently and this balances out the increased consumption with higher supply. As Plato well put it: "necessity is the mother of invention".

Production over human time, therefore, will repeat to be the most fundamental metric in deciding what makes something or anything a good or bad medium of store of value. 

## Bitcoin as a store of value

> "It is the mark of an educated mind to be able to entertain a thought without accepting it." - Artistotle

Because of the prevalent Malthusian beliefs that resource is scarce, arguments against Bitcoin as a store of value can be formulated as the following:

- Many physical assets like gold or silver are equally scarce and take great human efforts to produce, so these physical assets provide stronger vehicles to store value than Bitcoin OR
- Bitcoin lacks intrinsic value OR
- Bitcoin's price is highly volatile OR
- Bitcoin ownership is most concentrated and held by a small number of individuals and entities, often known as "whales"

We shall entertain and examine each of these thoughts carefully.
